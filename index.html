Chapter one · Block 1 — Strategy & Goals

01What is digital marketing — and how do we set goals?

"Digital marketing" is not a separate discipline. It is marketing done through channels where the customer talks back.

The opening session reframes a common confusion: digital is a channel, not a separate kind of marketing. The activity stays the same — create, communicate, deliver and exchange value. What changes is that the flow becomes bidirectional (Company ↔ Consumers), price becomes dynamic, products can be co-created, and the customer journey is roughly 70% digital.

The frameworks worth knowing cold

SMART goals Doran (1981) Core
A vague intention is not a goal. A SMART goal is specific, measurable, actionable, relevant and time-based.
  • Specific — answer the 5 W's (who/what/when/where/why)
  • Measurable — what metric tells you it worked
  • Actionable — realistic given existing baselines
  • Relevant — tied to business objectives, not vanity
  • Time-based — a clear deadline ("within 3 months")
  • Exam move — never say "increase brand awareness". Say "raise top-of-mind recall by 5% in Q4 vs Q3 baseline".
Meaningful Brands Havas Media Group Key
75% of brands could vanish without consumers caring. Meaningful brands clear that bar by activating personal, collective and functional value.
  • Personal benefits — physical, intellectual, social, emotional, financial, organisational, natural
  • Collective benefits — ethics, environment, social justice, economy, employer brand
  • Functional benefits — quality, service, innovation, tech (does what it promises)
  • Implication — 48% of branded content is irrelevant. Volume ≠ communication. Quality of value matters more.
Value Ladder Used in class Core
Brand perception is a ladder built from product attributes up. Pull the bottom rung and the rest collapses.
  • RTB at the base — if the product fails technically, the whole ladder collapses (LEGO that breaks in 2 days)
  • WhatsApp example — emotional benefit = "everyone is there" (community + belonging)
  • Applies B2C and B2B — Volvo trucks: precision attribute → stability benefit → trust emotion → safety value
  • Changing perception — start by changing the attributes. Perception is sticky.
Goals funnel · Awareness → Lead → Conversion → Retention Class framework Core
Each rung needs a different message, channel and metric.
  • Awareness — they don't know you. Build the brand emotionally. Paid channels dominate. KPI: top-of-mind recall, brand mentions. Example: Dove Real Beauty Sketches — no product shown.
  • Lead generation — capture interest. Newsletter pop-ups, signups, Volvo's split-truck stability ad (B2B impact).
  • Conversion — turn lead into payer. Discounts, retargeting, easy checkout (PayPal cuts cart abandonment).
  • Retention vs Churn — keep them engaged (repeat purchase, email opens) vs lose them (unsubs, never return). Acquiring is far more expensive than retaining.
"Digital marketing does not exist — digital is a channel. The marketing we know stays the same; the delivery tools change." Class principle · Session 1

Cases that show up in MCQs

  • Dove · Real Beauty Sketches — awareness through emotional connection (not conversion)
  • Brussels Airlines · Going the Extra Mile — 15-year brand consistency
  • Volvo Trucks · Split — Van Damme balance, B2B precision attribute → stability benefit
  • LEGO — full ladder example (attributes = quality bricks → values = creativity)

Exam moves

  • If asked "what is digital marketing", say the use of digital and social channels to promote a brand or reach consumers — and emphasise that it is bidirectional.
  • Never reduce marketing to "the 4 Ps". The exam tests the customer-side reframe: 4 Cs = Customer solution, Cost, Communication, Convenience.
  • If asked about Havas Meaningful Brands, hit the headline number: 75% of brands could disappear.
  • If asked about goal-setting, anchor on SMART and apply the 5 W's.
  • For the Value Ladder, always lead with: "the bottom rung is the reason to believe — pull it and the whole brand collapses".
Chapter two · Block 2 — Journey & Media Planning

02The customer journey — and how to plan media around it

The journey is no longer linear, and that breaks the old funnel. Today it loops.

Session 2 maps the journey twice — the traditional linear funnel (Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Loyalty) and the social customer journey, where the consumer holds the power and the brand controls less of the message. Then it asks the operational question: which channel, for which audience, at which moment?

Mindmap — the territory of Block 2
Customer Journey + Media Plan
Traditional journey
  • Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Loyalty
  • Brand controls the message
  • Few channels, limited info
Social journey
  • Consumers loop, search, ask peers
  • Brand influences, doesn't control
  • Reviews and UGC redirect decisions
Media planning principles
  • The right audience
  • The right instrument
  • The right time + the right channel
Segmentation
  • Existing customers (retain)
  • New potential customers (acquire)
Linear vs. Social Customer Journey Class framework Core
Old: a tidy funnel. New: a loop where customers search peers before they search the brand.
  • Traditional — Need recognition → Information search → Alternatives → Purchase → Post-purchase
  • Social — Pre-purchase research happens on review sites, social media, peer messaging — long before the brand gets to speak.
  • Loyalty phase — DTC and retention strategies skip the front of the funnel: returning customer = lower acquisition cost.
  • Exam move — when the question contrasts "traditional vs digital", anchor on bidirectionality, customer control, and multi-channel.
Principles of media planning Class framework Key
A media plan answers four questions before it spends a euro.
  • Which audiences resonate most — segment first, broadcast later
  • Right channel — different goals live on different platforms (B2B → LinkedIn, mass awareness → TikTok/Instagram)
  • Right instrument — match tactic to goal (display ad ≠ paid search ≠ influencer)
  • Right time — what's the customer's stage in their journey?
  • Metric — must be measurable on the chosen channel (each channel has its own KPIs)

Owned vs Paid vs Earned — the trade-off table

  • Owned · ✅ full control · ❌ slow build, audience-investment required
  • Paid · ✅ instant reach, controllable message · ❌ users skip / block / mistrust
  • Earned · ✅ trustworthiness, perceived authenticity · ❌ no control — bad reviews can damage reputation

Exam moves

  • If asked about loyalty stages, say retention focuses on direct repurchase — bypassing acquisition is the highest-ROI lever.
  • Don't confuse "earned" with "free". Earned media costs investments in building the audience and brand worth talking about.
  • For media planning questions, mention segmentation + right audience + right channel + right time + measurable metric as a 4-part answer.
Chapter three · Block 3 — Social Media & Analytics

03Social media planning — Stakeholder Matrix, 3H content, KPIs

Three elements (stakeholders, platforms, content) plus a fourth layer (social advertising) — measured against four KPIs.

Sessions 3 and 4 belong together: how to plan social media, and how to measure it. The strategy rests on a triangle (stakeholders + platforms + content), is amplified by social advertising, organised in a 3H content plan (Help / Hub / Hero), and rolled up into the ROI equation: Traffic × CR% × AVG × Repetition.

3H Content Strategy Google/YouTube Core
All branded content fits into three tiers: Help (everyday), Hub (community-growth), Hero (mass-reach moments).
  • Help — low cost, high frequency, for existing fans. UGC and educational content (Heura, Caterpillar's iAmCaterpillar, Patagonia community photos).
  • Hub — community growth: giveaways (follow + like + tag = exponential reach), influencer partnerships (Warby Parker, Sephora Squad), paid community ads.
  • Hero — high investment, occasional, mass attention. Qatar Airways × Messi; Patagonia's Don't Buy This Jacket; Heura's "CARNE" billboards in Madrid.
  • Key rule — Hero without Help and Hub is unsustainable: there is nothing to retain the audience after the peak.
Stakeholder Contact Matrix Class framework Key
A grid mapping each audience to top goal, top platform, paid investment, key messages and priority stars.
  • Customers Seg. 1 — Top goal: Branding · Top platform: Instagram · Paid: $$ · ★★★★
  • Customers Seg. 2 — Top goal: Sales · Top platform: Facebook · Paid: $$$ · ★★★
  • Journalists/Bloggers — Top goal: Branding · Top platform: X (Twitter) · ★
  • Influencers/Agencies — Top goal: Branding · Top platform: Instagram + LinkedIn · ★★
  • Potential talent — Top goal: Branding · Top platform: LinkedIn · ★
  • Exam move — when asked about social media strategy, never skip stakeholder definition. Goals follow stakeholders, not the other way around.
Digital Marketing KPI Model Class framework Core
Four KPIs drive the commercial performance of any digital business.
  • Traffic — volume coming to your asset. No traffic = no opportunity.
  • CR% (Conversion rate) — visitors who complete the desired action. Small CR% gains have huge revenue impact.
  • AVG — average order value. Drives revenue per converted user.
  • Repetition — repeat purchases. Acquiring is far more expensive than retaining → highest-ROI lever.
  • Formula — ROI = (Revenue − Cost) / Cost × 100%. Traffic × CR% = conversions; × AVG = revenue; × Repetition = CLV.
"Brand-building compounds over time and lifts the ceiling. Performance-only hits a ceiling — the Ramp & Bump." Class principle · Adidas / Nike / Airbnb cases

Real cases from the slides

  • Heura Foods — plant-based brand, full 3H ecosystem (Help: educational posts; Hub: giveaways; Hero: provocative outdoor)
  • KLM — Twitter customer support in real time (CS goal)
  • Starbucks · My Starbucks Idea — crowdsourced R&D platform
  • Patagonia — UGC reposts + "Don't Buy This Jacket" Hero
  • Adidas — admitted over-investing in performance, neglected brand

Exam moves

  • When asked about social media strategy, lead with the triangle: stakeholders + platforms + content, then add social advertising as a fourth layer.
  • If asked about content frequency, anchor on 3H: Help (high), Hub (medium), Hero (low).
  • Don't memorise KPIs as a list. Show them as a chain: Traffic × CR% × AVG, with Repetition looping back to multiply CLV.
  • For the Ramp & Bump question, name brands: Adidas and Nike admitted the error; Airbnb shifted.
Chapter four · Block 4 — Paid Media

04Paid media — buying attention, by channel, by intent

Paid media is not just "ads". It is bought distribution against measurable goals — search vs display vs social, each with its own logic.

Session 6 reframes paid media against owned and earned, then drills into the channel taxonomy. The principle: pay for the audience you can't reach organically, but only if the channel matches the goal.

Mindmap — the territory of Block 4
Paid media · channel + creative + objective
Paid search (SEM)
  • Google Ads, Bing Ads
  • Auction-based: bid + Quality Score
  • High intent — transactional queries
Display
  • Banner ads on third-party sites
  • Lower intent — interruption-based
  • Visual creative, retargeting common
Social ads
  • Meta, TikTok, LinkedIn, X, Pinterest
  • Objectives: Awareness, Traffic, Leads, Sales
  • Maps directly to B/S goal codes
Programmatic
  • Automated buying at scale
  • RTB (real-time bidding)
Paid vs Owned vs Earned — the trade-off matrix Class framework Core
Three media types, three different cost/control/credibility profiles.
  • Paid — fast, controllable, scalable; but users skip/block, ads have lower trust.
  • Owned — full control of message and brand; but audiences need to be built (slow), maintenance-heavy.
  • Earned — most trusted (perceived authentic); but uncontrolled — bad press, viral negativity.
  • Exam move — when asked which to use, the answer is rarely "one". The answer is the balance, tied to the goal at each funnel stage.
Meta campaign objectives Meta Ads Manager Supporting
Each campaign objective maps to a goal (Branding or Sales).
  • Awareness — Branding · build reach
  • Traffic — Branding/Sales bridge · drive to site
  • Engagement — Branding · likes, shares
  • Leads — Sales · form fills, sign-ups
  • App promotion — Sales · installs, in-app actions
  • Sales — Sales · purchases / conversions

Practical decisions when designing a paid creative

  • Intent — match the creative to the journey stage (mass awareness ≠ retargeting CTA)
  • Channel native fit — TikTok vertical / Pinterest pinnable / LinkedIn professional
  • Hook in 3 seconds — mobile attention drops fast
  • Clear CTA — one ask per ad
  • Measurement plan before launch — what does success look like in numbers?

Exam moves

  • Don't confuse SEM (paid search) with SEO (organic search). One is paid; the other is owned.
  • For paid social campaigns, remember the objective ladder mirrors the funnel: Awareness → Traffic → Leads → Sales.
  • If asked why paid media has lower trust than earned, the answer is controlled message + obvious commercial intent.
Chapter five · Block 5 — SEO

05SEO — winning organic traffic without paying for the click

Three pillars (relevance, popularity, user behaviour) sit on a foundation of technical hygiene. Add E-A-T for trust.

Session 7 covers SEO fundamentals — Google's PageRank legacy, modern ranking factors, the SERP anatomy, technical SEO, and the new wrinkle: optimizing for LLM answers (AEO / GEO). The principle: organic results are trusted more than paid, and SEO is the only channel where consistent traffic compounds for free.

SEO = Relevance + Popularity + User Behaviour + Technical

  • Long-tail = easier to rank, more qualified
  • Freshness · ccTLD geo-targeting

  • Mentions · social signals · brand searches
  • Quality > quantity for backlinks

  • Critical for YMYL sites (health, money)

  • Advanced: featured snippet · PLAs · knowledge cards · local pack · news box · related Qs
Technical SEO
  • HTTPS · page speed · sitemaps · robots.txt
  • Optimised URLs (hyphens, not underscores)
  • 301 vs 302 redirects

Keyword Intent — 4 types Search intent framework Core
Search queries reveal where the user is in the journey. Match content to intent.
  • Informational — research/browsing ("how to choose running shoes")
  • Navigational — finding a specific brand ("Nike running shoes")
  • Commercial — comparing options ("best running shoes for endurance")
  • Transactional — ready to buy ("running shoes sale online")
  • Long-tail — specific multi-word queries with high CTR and lower competition. Best target for new sites.
Technical SEO checklist Google Webmaster Guidelines Key
Seven non-content elements that make or break your rankings.
  • 1. HTTPS — official ranking signal since 2014. Mandatory.
  • 2. Page speed — 25% leave after ~4s; +1s delay → −16% satisfaction. Direct ranking factor.
  • 3. Robots & sitemaps — XML sitemap, href links, robots.txt. Cloaking = penalty.
  • 4. URLs — keyword-focused, unique, short, hyphens (not underscores). 301 (permanent, passes authority) vs 302 (temporary, doesn't).
  • 5. Canonicalization — rel=canonical for duplicate content.
  • 6. Structured data — Schema.org via JSON-LD (Google's preferred format). Enables rich snippets.
  • 7. Mobile-first — Google indexes mobile version primarily.
E-A-T Google Quality Raters Guidelines Key
Especially load-bearing for YMYL ("Your Money Your Life") — health, finance, legal, safety.
  • Expertise — author with genuine subject knowledge
  • Authoritativeness — site recognised in the field
  • Trustworthiness — secure, transparent, accurate

AI is reshaping SEO — the new acronyms

  • Hybrid wins — Reisenbichler 2022 (Marketing Science): humans + AI for content beats human-only and AI-only. ~91% lower labour cost; no readability loss.
  • oLLM traffic — Kaiser & Schulze 2025: ChatGPT referrals convert better than paid social but worse than other traditional channels.
  • AEO / GEO — Answer / Generative Engine Optimization. The new layer on top of SEO: optimize for LLM citations by being authoritative, clear, well-structured.

Exam moves

  • If asked about ranking factors, name three pillars: relevance + popularity + user behaviour. Add technical SEO as the foundation.
  • For keyword strategy, mention long-tail keywords for new sites: less competition, higher CTR, more qualified traffic.
  • If asked about backlinks, the rule is quality over quantity + avoid black-hat schemes.
  • Distinguish 301 (permanent + passes authority) from 302 (temporary + does not). This is a classic distractor pair.

Content Format Matrix Smart Insights · class framework Core
Two axes: cognitive approach (emotional ↔ rational) and journey goal (awareness ↔ purchase).
  • Pick by stage — there is no universally best format. Choose by cognitive approach + journey stage.
  • Mid-range formats — articles, eBooks, eNews sit at the centre of the matrix (mixed goals).
Storytelling archetypes Class framework Key
From simplest to most complex: Performer → Promoter → Professor → Poet.
  • Performer — "I want you to feel". Pure emotional stimulus. Anti-cruelty ads, fundraising.
  • Promoter — "I want you to say yes". Old world → Trigger → New world. Product launches.
  • Professor — "I want to change your view". Challenge → Implications → Why this → Thesis. B2B case studies, thought leadership.
  • Poet — "I want to change your belief". Full arc: Human → Desire → Constriction → Resistance → Adventures → Relationships → Truth. Brand documentaries, social change.
Valuable Content Checklist Class framework Core
Five dimensions every piece of content must satisfy before publishing.
  • Findable — h1 tag, metadata, ≥2 links in body, alt-tags on images
  • Readable — inverted-pyramid, bullets, headers, chunking
  • Understandable — user personas reflected, right format, intro for context
  • Actionable — call to action, share invitation, related links, comments
  • Shareable — emotional hook, share widgets, hashtag-ready
3 Revenue Models for Content Lambrecht et al. (2014) Supporting
Content monetisation falls into three buckets, each with its own risk profile.
  • 1. Selling content — memberships, products/services, freemium (Patreon, Ko-fi)
  • 2. Selling information — user-data sale (raises GDPR/ethical concerns)
  • 3. Selling eyeballs — advertising on owned channels (Reddit-style sponsored content)

The five ways content backfires

  • No strategy → attracts bad leads
  • Inaccessible → churn (1bn people globally with disabilities)
  • Repetitive → cognitive overload, disengagement
  • Volume over value → wasted spend
  • Poor maintenance → outdated content erodes credibility + SEO

Exam moves

  • If asked which content format to use, never give a single answer. Anchor on the matrix axes: cognitive approach × journey stage.
  • For brand narrative questions, distinguish brand story (timeless, aspirational, not SMART) from brand's stories (individual pieces, SMART-anchored).
  • Memorise the five checklist dimensions in order: Findable, Readable, Understandable, Actionable, Shareable.
  • Lambrecht's three revenue models are a classic essay scaffold: content / information / eyeballs.
Closing · MCQ exam tips

★Twelve rules to attack the MCQ

The pattern of distractors in this exam is consistent. These rules cover most of the traps.

1 — Read the question carefully when "NOT" or "FALSE" appears

A material fraction of stems are negatively framed. Misreading the negation is the most common avoidable error.

2 — Differentiate close neighbours

  • 4 Ps vs 4 Cs — Product/Price/Place/Promotion vs Customer solution/Cost/Communication/Convenience
  • Blue Ocean vs Red Ocean — non-customers + new demand vs current customers + existing demand
  • VR vs AR vs MR — VR replaces reality; AR enhances it; MR sits between
  • Global vs International vs Local brands — most companies use a combination
  • SEO vs SEM — owned vs paid

3 — "Both A and B" / "Neither A nor B" — usually the answer when both individual options sound plausible

This is the professor's favourite pattern. If both A and B feel correct, pick "A and B are correct".

4 — Innovation is about value, not just newness

If asked "what is innovation from a marketing perspective", the answer involves superior value proposition vs the competition or creating a new pattern of consumption, not "a new product no matter what".

5 — GloCal = balance, not a marketing-mix trick

GloCal strategy means balancing global and local. It is NOT "using a global mix in local markets".

6 — The Reckitt Benckiser growth model

RB's key point in the slides is investing in innovation and brand equity. Not "only global brands" and not "only 20 fully local brands".

7 — Lenovo's "blind strategy"

Volume-first to extract better supplier costs — not "improving quality" or "focus groups". The innovation factor: listening to customers AND understanding how they'll use the tech.

8 — Why did VR fail in the 90s?

Neither users nor designers were ready. Not "product wasn't ready" alone or "customer wasn't ready" alone.

9 — Value equation = benefits ÷ sacrifices

Value is the relationship between perceived benefits and perceived sacrifices. NOT the relationship to a category-average price.

10 — The marketplace today

Competition is global AND customers are demanding/sophisticated → the answer is "A and B are correct". Almost always.

11 — Marketing managers focus on…

Both current customers AND non-customers. Never one alone. Same pattern as #10.

12 — Length parity check

If three options are roughly the same length and one is much longer or much shorter, suspect a leak — that one is often the correct answer (the professor explained too much) or the obvious distractor (over-specified to trap). Sanity-check against the framework.

"If both individual options sound right, the answer is 'A and B are correct'. If neither sounds right, the answer is 'Neither A nor B'." Pattern observed across the real test
